# Professional Skills Module – Complete Learning Journey

> *“Professional skills shape not only how we work — but who we become in the workplace.”*

# ✍️ Lesson 2: CV & Cover Letter Writing

**Learning Outcome 2:** Recognizing CV structure, writing cover letters

## Understanding What a CV Really Is

According to the lesson, a CV — or *Curriculum Vitae* — is a **comprehensive summary** of who we are as candidates: our qualifications, experiences, skills, achievements, and strengths.

I learned the difference between:

- **CV:** detailed, multi-section document
- **Resume:** shorter, summary-style document

We also discussed global differences: how in the USA & Europe, CVs and resumes differ — while in countries like Sri Lanka, India, and Australia, the terms are often interchangeable.

## The Structure of an Effective CV

The lesson emphasized that a well-structured CV is not just readable — it is **visually appealing, consistent, and strategic**.

### A strong CV includes:

- **Personal Details**
- **Professional Summary**
- **Work Experience**
- **Education**
- **Skills (Technical + Soft)**
- **Achievements & Awards**
- **Projects**
- **Referees**

What stood out to me was how every section should be shaped around what *employers* are looking for — not just what we want to say about ourselves.

## Tailoring the CV – What Makes It Stand Out

One of the biggest lessons was learning the difference between a **generic CV** and a **tailored CV**.
A tailored CV:

- Uses keywords from the job description
- Reflects the company's needs
- Highlights the most relevant skills
- Shows you understand the role

This completely changed how I approach CV writing. Instead of sending the same document everywhere, I now adjust it depending on the job — whether it's a technical role, design-focused position, or leadership opportunity.

> *“A tailored CV shows intention — and intention shows professionalism.”*

## What NOT to Include in a CV

The lesson also highlighted common mistakes students often make, such as:

- Irrelevant personal details
- Unprofessional email addresses
- Overly long descriptions
- Inconsistent formatting
- Including photos unnecessarily
- Typos and grammar errors

Learning what *not* to include was just as important as learning what to include.

## Cover Letter Writing – Making the First Impression Count

Just as important as the CV was learning how to write a **professional cover letter** tailored to a job.

I learned that a cover letter should:

- Introduce yourself
- Show why you're applying
- Prove you understand the company
- Highlight your best achievements
- End with a confident closing

The example cover letter activity helped me identify:

- The function of each paragraph
- How to express my strengths
- How to show genuine interest in a role

This lesson changed how I approach every application — now I understand that a cover letter is not optional; it is a **powerful positioning tool**.

> *“A cover letter is your voice — it tells the employer why you matter.”*
